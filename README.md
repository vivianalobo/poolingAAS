# Bayesian Joint Graduation of Mortality Tables for Multiple Populations with Age-Varying Smoothness


Authors: Luiz F. V. de Figueiredo, Viviana G. R. Lobo,  Mariane B. Alves and Thais C. O. Fonseca

Reliable mortality graduation is a key element of actuarial work, forming the basis for pricing, reserving, capital modelling, and longevity risk assessment. In practice, actuaries are often required to work with mortality data disaggregated by product, gender, capital, or region, which can result in sparse or incomplete information in some subpopulations and age ranges. Estimating mortality tables separately in such settings can lead to unstable graduations and excessive uncertainty, particularly at older ages.
This paper introduces a Bayesian smoothing framework for the joint graduation of mortality rates across multiple related populations. The proposed approach builds on the well-known equivalence between penalised splines and Gaussian state-space representations, embedding spline smoothing within a broader class of multivariate Bayesian dynamical linear models. In this formulation, smoothness across ages arises from structured stochastic dependence between neighbouring ages, while the state-space structure provides a fully probabilistic and easily extensible modelling framework. By allowing selected latent components to be shared across populations, the model enables effective pooling of information while preserving meaningful differences between mortality curves. This general representation facilitates coherent multivariate extensions, such as common latent terms, that are more naturally specified and interpreted than in traditional multivariate spline approaches, and provides a principled framework for assessing convergence hypotheses across populations.
A central feature of the model is the use of age-specific discount factors as tuning parameters that regulate the trade-off between responsiveness to new experience and stability of mortality assumptions. Lower discounting at younger ages allows greater responsiveness to new information, while higher discounting at adult and older ages promotes stability in regions where exposure is typically limited.
